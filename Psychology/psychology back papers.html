Q1. (A) Define Psychology & Subject matter. (B) Origin and historical background.

14 MARKS

(A) Definition and Subject Matter (7 Marks)

  • Definition: Psychology originates from two ancient Greek words: "Psyche" (meaning mind/soul) and "Logos" (meaning study). Today, it is formally defined as the scientific study of human and animal behavior and mental processes.
  • Behavior: Refers to any overt action that can be observed or measured. This includes everything from physical movements (walking, talking) to complex actions (social interactions, problem-solving).
  • Mental Processes: These are internal, covert activities of the mind that cannot be directly observed but are inferred from behavior. They include thinking, memory, perception, and decision-making.
  • Emotions and Personality: Psychology studies affective states (happiness, anger, fear) and how they influence behavior. It also explores personality—the unique, enduring patterns of thinking and behaving that make each individual distinct.
  • Biological Bases and Development: It deeply investigates how the brain, nervous system, and genetics dictate our behavior. It also tracks the physical, emotional, and cognitive growth of humans from birth to old age.

(B) Origin and Historical Background (7 Marks)

  • Ancient Roots (3000 BCE - 500 CE): The foundation of psychology traces back to ancient civilizations. Philosophers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle engaged in early debates concerning the nature of consciousness, rationality, and whether knowledge is innate or learned through experience.
  • Middle Ages and Renaissance: During the Middle Ages, behavior was often attributed to spiritual forces. During the Renaissance, Thinker René Descartes proposed Mind-Body Dualism—the concept that the physical body and immaterial mind are separate but interact. John Locke later introduced Empiricism, arguing knowledge comes from sensory experience.
  • The Birth of Modern Psychology (1879): Psychology transitioned into an independent scientific discipline when Wilhelm Wundt established the world's first formal psychology laboratory at the University of Leipzig, Germany. He utilized experimental methods like introspection.
  • Major Schools of Thought: Following Wundt's Structuralism, William James developed Functionalism in America. The 20th century saw the rise of Sigmund Freud's Psychoanalysis (focusing on the unconscious) and John B. Watson's Behaviorism (focusing only on observable actions), shaping modern psychology.

Q2. (A) Two influential schools of psychology. (B) Main subfields and their functions.

14 MARKS

(A) Two Most Influential Schools of Psychology (7 Marks)

  • 1. Psychoanalysis (Sigmund Freud): Founded by the Austrian psychiatrist Sigmund Freud, this school revolutionized psychology by shifting focus to the unconscious mind. Freud proposed that human behavior is profoundly driven by hidden primal urges, unfulfilled desires, repressed traumatic memories, and unresolved childhood conflicts.

    He theorized that the psyche consists of the Id (primal desires), Ego (rational mediator), and Superego (moral conscience). Psychoanalysts use techniques like dream interpretation and free association to heal neurotic patients by bringing hidden conflicts into conscious awareness.
  • 2. Behaviourism (John B. Watson & B.F. Skinner): Emerging in the early 20th century as a strict rebellion against the unobservable "mind," Behaviorists argued that psychology must focus exclusively on observable, measurable behavior.

    They posited that humans are born as "blank slates" (Tabula Rasa) and that all complex behavior is entirely learned through environmental interaction and conditioning mechanisms (rewards and punishments). Behaviorism brought immense scientific rigor to psychology.

(B) Main Subfields of Psychology and Their Functions (7 Marks)

  • Clinical Psychology: The largest applied subfield. Its primary function is the comprehensive assessment, diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of mental, emotional, and behavioral disorders (e.g., anxiety, depression, schizophrenia) using psychotherapy.
  • Cognitive Psychology: Functions to study internal mental processes. It explores how the human mind acquires, processes, stores, and retrieves information, extensively covering memory, attention span, logical reasoning, and language.
  • Developmental Psychology: Functions to study how and why human beings change over the course of their entire lifespan. It covers infancy, childhood cognitive development, adolescence, and the psychological impacts of aging.
  • Social Psychology: Investigates how individuals' thoughts, feelings, and actions are deeply influenced by society and the presence of others. It functions to explain group dynamics, peer pressure, prejudice, and leadership.
  • Industrial-Organizational (I/O) Psychology: Applies psychological principles directly to the workplace. Its function is to enhance employee productivity, ensure fair hiring practices, improve occupational safety, and foster positive organizational cultures.

Q3. (A) Perceptual organization and principles. (B) Extrasensory Perception (ESP) and types.

14 MARKS

(A) Perceptual Organization & Its Principles (7 Marks)

  • Concept: Perceptual organization is the process by which the human brain automatically arranges and makes sense of the raw sensory information we receive. Gestalt psychologists proposed that our brains organize elements into meaningful "wholes" rather than seeing random, fragmented parts.

  • Principle of Similarity: The brain automatically groups elements together that share visually similar characteristics, such as color, shape, or size.
  • Principle of Proximity (Nearness): Objects situated physically close to one another are perceived as belonging to the same unified group.
  • Principle of Continuity: Our brain prefers to see smooth, continuous patterns rather than disconnected or jagged ones.
  • Principle of Closure: The brain mentally fills in missing visual gaps to perceive a complete, whole object even if parts are obscured.

(B) Extrasensory Perception (ESP) and Its Types (7 Marks)

  • Definition: Extrasensory Perception (ESP) refers to the controversial, claimed ability to receive information beyond the five known physical senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell). While popular in fiction, science does not confirm ESP as a proven ability.
  • 1. Telepathy: Often described as "mind reading," it is the claimed ability to transfer thoughts, feelings, or ideas from one person to another without using words or physical communication.
  • 2. Clairvoyance: Meaning "clear seeing," it refers to the ability to perceive distant objects, events, or hidden places without using normal senses.
  • 3. Precognition: The alleged ability to foresee or predict future events before they actually happen, often claimed to occur through vivid dreams or intense gut feelings.
  • 4. Psychokinesis (Telekinesis): The claimed ability to move, bend, or manipulate physical objects using only the power of the mind, without any physical contact.

(A) The Process of Perception in Detail (7 Marks)

  • Overview: While Sensation is the passive biological intake of physical energy, Perception is the active psychological process of making sense of that sensory data. It occurs continuously in three primary steps:

  • 1. Selection (Attention): At any given moment, our sensory organs are bombarded by an overwhelming amount of environmental stimuli. Because the brain has limited processing capacity, it engages in selective attention—filtering out irrelevant background noise and selecting only the most crucial or intense details to focus on.
  • 2. Organization: The selected sensory signals do not remain as chaotic fragments. The brain rapidly and automatically arranges these signals into cohesive, structured patterns. Using Gestalt principles, the brain groups information to form whole, recognizable objects instead of visual static.
  • 3. Interpretation: After organizing the pattern, the brain must assign it meaning. Interpretation is highly subjective and relies heavily on an individual's past experiences, cultural background, current emotional state, and expectations (perceptual set).

(B) Factors That Influence Perception (7 Marks)

  • Past Experiences and Expectations: What we have encountered and learned previously fundamentally dictates how we interpret new stimuli. Our Perceptual Set (expectations) causes the brain to actively construct perception to match what we anticipate seeing.
  • Needs and Motivation: Our current physiological or psychological drives alter perception. For example, a starving person is significantly more likely to notice food advertisements than a fully fed person.
  • Emotions and Mood: Strong feelings heavily distort interpretation. Intense fear can cause a person to organize a harmless shadow into a threatening figure. Happiness makes neutral facial expressions appear friendlier.
  • Cultural Background: Different cultures organize and interpret colors, symbols, gestures, and social cues in drastically different ways.
  • External Stimulus Factors: The physical characteristics of the stimulus itself—such as high intensity (brightness/loudness), massive size, sharp contrast, and rapid movement—naturally command attention and force specific perceptual grouping.

Q5. (A) Observational learning & conditions. (B) Role of nature in classical conditioning.

14 MARKS

(A) Observational Learning and Its Conditions (7 Marks)

  • Definition: Pioneered by Albert Bandura, observational learning posits that humans can acquire new, complex behaviors simply by watching others (models) and observing the consequences of their actions, without needing direct, hands-on reinforcement.
  • For observational learning to successfully manifest as a permanent behavior, four cognitive conditions must be met sequentially:
  • 1. Attention: The learner must actively pay close, focused attention to the model's behavior. If the learner is distracted, the behavior cannot be cognitively processed. Attractive or authoritative models command the most attention.
  • 2. Retention: The learner must possess the memory capacity to securely store a mental representation of what the model did. Without cognitive retention, the learner cannot replicate the action once the model departs.
  • 3. Reproduction: The learner must possess the necessary physical and mental capabilities to translate the stored memory into actual physical action.
  • 4. Motivation: The learner must have a strong desire or incentive to perform the behavior. This is typically fueled by vicarious reinforcement—seeing the model receive a reward for their actions.

(B) The Role of Nature in Classical Conditioning (7 Marks)

  • Reliance on Innate Reflexes: Classical conditioning cannot create entirely new behaviors out of thin air. Instead, it relies completely on exploiting deep, biological, unlearned reflexes provided by "nature." The Unconditioned Stimulus and Unconditioned Response (like salivating for food or blinking at a puff of air) are hardwired survival mechanisms. Conditioning simply substitutes a neutral trigger for these natural reflexes.
  • Biological Preparedness: Evolution and nature heavily dictate what associations we learn easily. Humans are naturally primed to rapidly develop conditioned fears toward natural evolutionary threats, such as snakes, spiders, or heights, to aid survival. However, it is extraordinarily difficult to condition a fear response toward a harmless object like a flower.
  • Limits on Learning (Taste Aversion): Classical conditioning has been shaped by evolution. If an animal eats a poisonous plant and becomes ill, nature ensures it forms a permanent association to avoid that taste after just a single bad experience. Furthermore, you cannot condition an animal to perform an action that violates its innate biological instincts.

Q6. (A) Pavlov's classical conditioning. (B) Reinforcement and punishment.

14 MARKS

(A) Pavlov's Experiment on Classical Conditioning (7 Marks)

  • Concept: Ivan Pavlov accidentally discovered classical conditioning while studying canine digestion. It is an associative learning process where an organism learns to powerfully connect a neutral environmental stimulus with a naturally occurring biological stimulus, creating a new involuntary reflex.
  • Before Conditioning (Natural Response): Pavlov observed that dogs naturally salivated when presented with meat powder. The food is the Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS) because it triggers an automatic reflex. The salivation is the Unconditioned Response (UCR). A ringing bell produced absolutely no salivation effect and was merely a Neutral Stimulus (NS).
  • During Conditioning (Acquisition): Pavlov rigorously paired the two stimuli by ringing the bell immediately before presenting the meat powder to the dogs. Over multiple, repeated trials, the dogs' brains began to forge a powerful neural association between the sound of the bell and the imminent arrival of food.
  • After Conditioning (Learned Response): Eventually, Pavlov rang the bell without presenting any food at all. The dogs began to salivate profusely solely at the sound of the bell. The bell had successfully transformed into a Conditioned Stimulus (CS), and the salivation triggered by it became a learned Conditioned Response (CR).
Operant Conditioning Matrix (B.F. Skinner)

Positive Reinforcement

ADD desirable stimulus to INCREASE behavior. (e.g., Giving a rat a food pellet for pressing a lever).

Negative Reinforcement

REMOVE undesirable stimulus to INCREASE behavior. (e.g., Turning off a painful electric grid).

Positive Punishment

ADD painful stimulus to DECREASE behavior. (e.g., Delivering an electric shock to stop behavior).

Negative Punishment

REMOVE pleasant stimulus to DECREASE behavior. (e.g., Taking away a teenager's smartphone).

(B) Role of Reinforcement and Punishment in Shaping Behavior (7 Marks)

  • Operant Conditioning Overview: Formulated by B.F. Skinner, operant conditioning proves that voluntary behavior is shaped, maintained, or extinguished entirely by the consequences that immediately follow the action.
  • Reinforcement (Increases Behavior): Reinforcement is any consequence that strengthens a behavior. Positive Reinforcement adds a rewarding stimulus (e.g., giving an employee a bonus for hard work). Negative Reinforcement removes an annoying or painful stimulus (e.g., putting on a seatbelt to stop a car's annoying alarm).
  • Punishment (Decreases Behavior): Punishment is any consequence that weakens a behavior. Positive Punishment adds an unpleasant stimulus (e.g., a police officer giving a speeding ticket). Negative Punishment removes a desirable stimulus (e.g., a teacher taking away a student's recess privileges).

Q7. (A) Working-Model Theory of memory. (B) Key aspects of Short-Term Memory.

14 MARKS

(A) Working-Model Theory of Memory with Examples (7 Marks)

  • Concept: Proposed by Baddeley and Hitch, the Working Memory Model replaced the idea of a simple, passive "Short-Term Memory" box. It describes a highly active, multi-component cognitive system used to temporarily store and manipulate information during complex tasks like reasoning, comprehension, and learning.
  • 1. The Central Executive: The "boss" of the system. It controls attention, coordinates the subsystems, and decides which information is important. Example: When driving a car while talking to a passenger, the central executive shifts your focus to the road when a hazard appears.
  • 2. The Phonological Loop: Handles auditory and verbal information. It acts like an inner voice repeating information. Example: Silently repeating a phone number to yourself until you can dial it.
  • 3. The Visuospatial Sketchpad: The "inner eye." It handles visual and spatial information, allowing you to manipulate images in your mind. Example: Visualizing the map of your house to calculate how many windows it has.
  • 4. The Episodic Buffer: A temporary backup storage that integrates visual, spatial, and verbal information with time sequencing, linking working memory to long-term memory.

(B) Key Aspects of Short-Term Memory in Detail (7 Marks)

  • Overview: Short-Term Memory (STM) is the active part of the memory system that holds the information we are currently thinking about or consciously processing.
  • 1. Limited Capacity: Unlike long-term memory, STM can only hold a very small, restricted amount of information at one time. Cognitive psychologist George Miller identified this strict capacity limit as the "Magic Number 7" (plus or minus 2 items). This is why phone numbers were historically 7 digits long.
  • 2. Brief Duration: Information stored in STM decays exceptionally fast. Without active mental repetition or manipulation, data stored in STM will naturally fade and be permanently lost in approximately 15 to 30 seconds.
  • 3. Maintenance Rehearsal: Because of its highly brief duration, the primary mechanism to keep information alive in STM is through active maintenance rehearsal (e.g., repeatedly reciting an OTP code out loud). If this active rehearsal loop is interrupted by a distraction, the information is instantly forgotten.

Q8. (A) Factors responsible for forgetting. (B) Relationship between brain and memory.

14 MARKS

(A) Factors Responsible for Forgetting (7 Marks)

  • Definition of Forgetting: The apparent loss, modification, or inability to successfully retrieve previously encoded and stored information from memory. It is a highly adaptive process that prevents the brain from being cluttered with useless data. Key factors include:
  • Trace Decay Theory: Proposes that memory leaves a physical "trace" in the brain. If this memory is not actively rehearsed or used over time, the neural trace simply fades away and decays naturally.
  • Interference Theory: Proposes that we forget because other, similar memories get in the way and block the retrieval pathway.
    - Proactive Interference: Older memories interfere with retrieving newer information (e.g., accidentally typing your old bank password instead of the new one).
    - Retroactive Interference: Newer memories interfere with retrieving older information (e.g., learning a new phone number makes you completely forget your old one).
  • Retrieval Failure (Cue-Dependent Forgetting): The memory is still in long-term storage, but the person lacks the right "cues" to access it. (e.g., the "Tip of the Tongue" phenomenon).
  • Motivated Forgetting (Repression): A psychoanalytic concept where the brain unconsciously pushes highly traumatic or distressing memories out of conscious awareness to protect the individual from anxiety.

(B) The Relationship Between Brain and Memory (7 Marks)

  • Neuropsychological Basis: Memory is not stored in one single "box" in the brain. It is distributed across complex neural networks. Learning and memory physically alter the structure of the brain by strengthening synaptic connections between neurons (a process known as Long-Term Potentiation).
  • The Hippocampus: This seahorse-shaped structure in the brain's temporal lobe is absolutely critical for forming new explicit memories (facts and events). It acts like a "save button," consolidating short-term memories into permanent long-term storage across the cerebral cortex.
  • The Amygdala: Situated next to the hippocampus, the amygdala processes emotions. It plays a critical role in forming highly emotional memories, explaining why we easily remember terrifying or extremely joyful events (like accidents or weddings) more vividly than neutral events.
  • The Cerebellum and Basal Ganglia: These regions are responsible for storing implicit, procedural memories—such as the motor skills required to ride a bicycle, type on a keyboard, or play a musical instrument, which do not require conscious recall.

(A) Application of Psychology in Science and Engineering (7 Marks)

  • Human Factors & Cognitive Engineering: Psychologists collaborate intensely with hardware and software engineers to design intuitive, user-friendly interfaces. They apply principles of "Ergonomics" to design physical workspaces that reduce worker fatigue and physical injury. By studying human attention limits, they design advanced decision-support systems that minimize catastrophic human errors in high-stakes environments.
  • Aerospace Engineering: Psychologists study pilot behavior, visual perception illusions, and rapid decision-making under extreme stress to develop superior flight training simulators. They also deeply investigate "spaceflight psychology"—understanding the psychological toll of isolation on astronauts to design safer, more comfortable spacecraft interiors.
  • Biomedical Engineering: Psychologists assist in designing highly intuitive medical devices that minimize user error by stressed doctors. They also play a major role in rehabilitation engineering, developing assistive technologies and advanced prosthetics that adapt to the psychological needs of disabled patients.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Working with computer scientists to develop AI and Machine Learning models that process information logically, mimic human problem-solving safely, and remain transparent, explainable, and free of embedded human cognitive biases.

(B) Brief Note on Two Senses (Vision and Hearing) (7 Marks)

  • Overview (Sensory Transduction): The common function of all senses is to convert a physical environmental stimulus into an electrical signal the nervous system can read—a process called sensory transduction.
  • Vision (Sense of Sight): Considered the most dominant human sense, vision is triggered by physical light waves (photons) entering the eye. The lens focuses this light onto the retina at the back of the eye. Specialized photoreceptor cells known as rods (for dim light) and cones (for color and sharp detail) convert the light energy into electrical neural signals. These are sent to the visual cortex. Vision is crucial for spatial coordination and reading.
  • Hearing (Auditory Perception): Hearing is triggered by mechanical sound waves traveling through the air. These waves enter the ear canal and vibrate the eardrum. These vibrations are amplified by tiny bones in the middle ear and sent into the fluid-filled cochlea. Hair cells within the cochlea convert the mechanical vibrations into nerve impulses interpreted as sound by the brain. It is vital for communication and acting as a danger alarm.

Q10. (A) What is learning? Characteristics. (B) Memory importance in life.

14 MARKS

(A) What is learning? Characteristics of learning (7 Marks)

  • Definition of Learning: In psychology, learning is defined as a relatively permanent change in an organism's behavior, knowledge, or capability that occurs as a direct result of experience, practice, or observation. It excludes temporary changes caused by fatigue or biological maturation.
  • Lifelong Process: Learning is not limited to childhood or formal schooling. It continues throughout an individual's entire life as they adapt to new environments and technologies.
  • Active Process: Learning does not happen passively. It requires conscious effort, attention, engagement, and practice. Individuals must actively think critically to apply what they learn in real life.
  • Intentional or Unintentional: Sometimes learning is purposeful (e.g., studying for an exam). Other times, it happens naturally and unintentionally (e.g., learning from a sudden mistake or observing a peer's behavior).
  • Builds on Previous Knowledge: New learning is often anchored to what a person already knows. It acts like building blocks, where advanced concepts connect to foundational knowledge.

(B) Define Memory & Its Importance in Personal and Professional Life (7 Marks)

  • Definition of Memory: Memory is the highly complex cognitive process that allows the brain to encode, store, retain, and subsequently retrieve information and past experiences.
  • Importance in Personal Life: Memory is the foundation of human identity. It allows us to recognize family and friends, recall cherished life events (episodic memory), and navigate our physical environment safely. Without memory, we would have no continuous sense of self, and we would be incapable of learning from our past mistakes or forming lasting social relationships.
  • Importance in Professional Life: Cognitive processes like memory are crucial for career success. Good memory allows professionals (like doctors, lawyers, and engineers) to retain vast amounts of complex technical information and apply past experiences to solve novel problems efficiently.
  • Decision Making and Communication: Memory enables rapid decision-making by allowing professionals to draw upon a database of past outcomes. Furthermore, semantic memory (vocabulary and facts) is essential for articulating ideas clearly, managing teams, and maintaining effective workplace communication.
