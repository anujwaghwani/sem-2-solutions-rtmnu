(a) Equivalence Relation

[7 M]
Problem: Let $X = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7\}$ and $R = \{(x, y) \mid x - y \text{ is divisible by } 3\}$. Show that $R$ is an equivalence relation.

To show that $R$ is an equivalence relation, we must prove it is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.

1. Reflexivity:

For any $x \in X$, consider $x - x = 0$.

Since $0 = 0 \times 3$, $0$ is divisible by $3$.

Therefore, $(x, x) \in R$ for all $x \in X$. Hence, $R$ is reflexive.

2. Symmetry:

Let $(x, y) \in R$. This means $x - y$ is divisible by $3$.

So, $x - y = 3k$ for some integer $k$.

Multiplying by $-1$, we get $-(x - y) = -3k \implies y - x = 3(-k)$.

Since $-k$ is an integer, $y - x$ is divisible by $3$.

Therefore, $(y, x) \in R$. Hence, $R$ is symmetric.

3. Transitivity:

Let $(x, y) \in R$ and $(y, z) \in R$.

This implies $x - y = 3k$ and $y - z = 3m$ (where $k, m$ are integers).

Adding the two equations: $(x - y) + (y - z) = 3k + 3m \implies x - z = 3(k + m)$

Since $(k + m)$ is an integer, $x - z$ is divisible by $3$.

Therefore, $(x, z) \in R$. Hence, $R$ is transitive.

Conclusion: Since $R$ is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive, it is an equivalence relation.

(b) Set Algebra Proofs

[7 M]
Problem: Prove: (i) $A - (B \cup C) = (A - B) \cap (A - C)$ and (ii) $A - (B \cap C) = (A - B) \cup (A - C)$.

Using the definition $X - Y = X \cap Y'$ and De Morgan's Laws for sets:

Proof of (i):

\begin{align*} A - (B \cup C) &= A \cap (B \cup C)' \quad \text{(Def. of Difference)} \\ &= A \cap (B' \cap C') \quad \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &= (A \cap A) \cap (B' \cap C') \quad \text{(Idempotent: } A = A \cap A \text{)} \\ &= (A \cap B') \cap (A \cap C') \quad \text{(Commutative & Associative)} \\ &= (A - B) \cap (A - C) \quad \text{(Def. of Difference)} \end{align*}

Proof of (ii):

\begin{align*} A - (B \cap C) &= A \cap (B \cap C)' \quad \text{(Def. of Difference)} \\ &= A \cap (B' \cup C') \quad \text{(De Morgan's Law)} \\ &= (A \cap B') \cup (A \cap C') \quad \text{(Distributive Law)} \\ &= (A - B) \cup (A - C) \quad \text{(Def. of Difference)} \end{align*}

(a) Mathematical Induction

[7 M]
Problem: Show that $1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + ... + n^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}, \quad n \ge 1$

Let $P(n): 1^2 + 2^2 + ... + n^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$

Step 1: Base Case

For $n=1$: LHS $= 1^2 = 1$. RHS $= \frac{1(1+1)(2(1)+1)}{6} = \frac{1(2)(3)}{6} = 1$. $P(1)$ is true.

Step 2: Inductive Hypothesis

Assume $P(k)$ is true: $1^2 + 2^2 + ... + k^2 = \frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6}$

Step 3: Inductive Step

Prove $P(k+1)$: LHS $= 1^2 + ... + k^2 + (k+1)^2$

\begin{align*} \text{LHS} &= \frac{k(k+1)(2k+1)}{6} + (k+1)^2 \quad \text{[Using Assumption]} \\ &= \frac{k(k+1)(2k+1) + 6(k+1)^2}{6} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)[k(2k+1) + 6(k+1)]}{6} \quad \text{[Factor out (k+1)]} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)[2k^2 + 7k + 6]}{6} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)}{6} \quad \text{[Factor quadratic]} \\ &= \frac{(k+1)((k+1)+1)(2(k+1)+1)}{6} = \text{RHS of } P(k+1) \end{align*}

By induction, $P(n)$ is true for all $n \ge 1$.

(a) Normal Subgroups Intersection

[7 M]
Problem: Show that intersection of any two normal subgroups of a group G is a normal subgroup of G.

Let $H$ and $K$ be two normal subgroups of a group $G$. We must prove that $H \cap K$ is a normal subgroup of $G$. This requires proving two parts: (1) $H \cap K$ is a subgroup, and (2) $H \cap K$ is normal.

Part 1: $H \cap K$ is a Subgroup

  • Since $H$ and $K$ are subgroups, the identity element $e \in H$ and $e \in K$. Therefore, $e \in H \cap K$, which means $H \cap K \neq \emptyset$.
  • Let $x, y \in H \cap K$. This implies $x, y \in H$ and $x, y \in K$.
  • Because $H$ is a subgroup, $xy^{-1} \in H$. Because $K$ is a subgroup, $xy^{-1} \in K$.
  • Thus, $xy^{-1} \in H \cap K$. By the One-Step Subgroup Test, $H \cap K$ is a subgroup of $G$.

Part 2: $H \cap K$ is Normal

  • To prove normality, we must show that for any $g \in G$ and any $x \in H \cap K$, the element $gxg^{-1} \in H \cap K$.
  • Let $g \in G$ and $x \in H \cap K$. This means $x \in H$ and $x \in K$.
  • Since $H$ is normal in $G$ ($H \triangleleft G$) and $x \in H$, $gxg^{-1} \in H$.
  • Since $K$ is normal in $G$ ($K \triangleleft G$) and $x \in K$, $gxg^{-1} \in K$.
  • Therefore, $gxg^{-1} \in H \cap K$.

Conclusion: Since $H \cap K$ is a subgroup and satisfies the normality condition, $H \cap K$ is a normal subgroup of $G$.

(b) Monoid of Matrices

[7 M]
Problem: Show that the set of matrices $A_\alpha = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\alpha & -\sin\alpha \\ \sin\alpha & \cos\alpha \end{bmatrix}$, $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$, form a monoid.

Let $S = \{A_\alpha \mid \alpha \in \mathbb{R}\}$. To show $S$ is a monoid under matrix multiplication, we must prove three properties: Closure, Associativity, and the existence of an Identity Element.

1. Closure Property:

Let $A_\alpha, A_\beta \in S$. Their product is:

\begin{align*} A_\alpha \cdot A_\beta &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\alpha & -\sin\alpha \\ \sin\alpha & \cos\alpha \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\beta & -\sin\beta \\ \sin\beta & \cos\beta \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta & -\cos\alpha\sin\beta - \sin\alpha\cos\beta \\ \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta & -\sin\alpha\sin\beta + \cos\alpha\cos\beta \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\alpha+\beta) & -\sin(\alpha+\beta) \\ \sin(\alpha+\beta) & \cos(\alpha+\beta) \end{bmatrix} \\ &= A_{\alpha+\beta} \end{align*}

Since $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$, their sum $\alpha+\beta \in \mathbb{R}$. Thus, $A_{\alpha+\beta} \in S$. The set is closed under multiplication.

2. Associativity:

Matrix multiplication is associative by default for all $n \times n$ matrices over real numbers. Therefore, $(A_\alpha \cdot A_\beta) \cdot A_\gamma = A_\alpha \cdot (A_\beta \cdot A_\gamma)$ holds true.

3. Identity Element:

Let $\alpha = 0$. Since $0 \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$A_0 = \begin{bmatrix} \cos 0 & -\sin 0 \\ \sin 0 & \cos 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I$$

The identity matrix $I$ belongs to the set $S$, and for any $A_\alpha \in S$, $A_\alpha \cdot I = I \cdot A_\alpha = A_\alpha$.

Conclusion: Since the set satisfies closure, associativity, and possesses an identity element, it forms a Monoid.

(a) Subgroup Test

[7 M]
Problem: A non-empty subset $H$ of a group $G$ is a subgroup of $G$ iff: (i) $a, b \in H \Rightarrow a*b \in H$, (ii) $a \in H \Rightarrow a^{-1} \in H$.

We need to prove the "if and only if" condition (Two-Step Subgroup Test).

Part 1 (Forward): If $H$ is a subgroup, then (i) and (ii) hold.

Suppose $H$ is a subgroup of $G$. By definition, a subgroup is itself a group under the operation inherited from $G$. Therefore, $H$ must be closed under the operation, so $a, b \in H \Rightarrow a*b \in H$ (Condition i holds). Furthermore, every element in a group must have an inverse within that group, so $a \in H \Rightarrow a^{-1} \in H$ (Condition ii holds).

Part 2 (Backward): If (i) and (ii) hold, then $H$ is a subgroup.

Suppose $H$ is a non-empty subset of $G$ satisfying conditions (i) and (ii). To prove $H$ is a subgroup, we must show it satisfies all four group axioms:

  • Closure: Given directly by condition (i) that $a*b \in H$.
  • Associativity: The elements of $H$ are also elements of $G$. Since the operation $*$ is associative in $G$, it is automatically associative in $H$.
  • Identity: Since $H$ is non-empty, let $a \in H$. By condition (ii), $a^{-1} \in H$. By condition (i) (closure), $a*a^{-1} \in H$. Since $a*a^{-1} = e$ (the identity of G), we have $e \in H$.
  • Inverse: Given directly by condition (ii) that for every $a \in H$, its inverse $a^{-1} \in H$.

Conclusion: Since all group axioms are satisfied, $H \le G$. The theorem is proved.

(b) Homomorphism Properties

[7 M]
Problem: If $f$ is a homomorphism of $G$ into $G'$, then: (i) $f(e) = e'$ where $e'$ is the identity element of $G'$, (ii) $f(x^{-1}) = [f(x)]^{-1}$, $\forall x \in G$.

Given that $f: G \to G'$ is a homomorphism, the fundamental property is $f(xy) = f(x)f(y)$ for all $x, y \in G$.

Proof of (i) $f(e) = e'$:

Let $e$ be the identity in $G$. We know that for any element in $G$, $e \cdot e = e$.

Apply the homomorphism $f$ to both sides:

\begin{align*} f(e \cdot e) &= f(e) \\ f(e) \cdot f(e) &= f(e) \quad \text{[By homomorphism property]} \end{align*}

In the group $G'$, $f(e)$ is an element. We can multiply both sides by its inverse $[f(e)]^{-1}$:

\begin{align*} f(e) \cdot f(e) \cdot [f(e)]^{-1} &= f(e) \cdot [f(e)]^{-1} \\ f(e) \cdot e' &= e' \\ \mathbf{f(e)} &\mathbf{= e'} \end{align*}

Proof of (ii) $f(x^{-1}) = [f(x)]^{-1}$:

For any $x \in G$, its inverse $x^{-1}$ satisfies $x \cdot x^{-1} = e$ and $x^{-1} \cdot x = e$.

Applying the homomorphism $f$:

\begin{align*} f(x \cdot x^{-1}) &= f(e) \\ f(x) \cdot f(x^{-1}) &= e' \quad \text{[Since } f(e) = e' \text{]} \end{align*}

Similarly, $f(x^{-1} \cdot x) = f(x^{-1}) \cdot f(x) = e'$.

This shows that $f(x^{-1})$ acts as the inverse for the element $f(x)$ in the group $G'$. By the uniqueness of inverses in a group:

$$f(x^{-1}) = [f(x)]^{-1}$$

Problem: Find the closed form of generating function for the following sequences: (i) 3, -3, 3, -3, 3, -3, ... (ii) 1, 0, -1, 0, 1, 0, -1, 0, ... (iii) 0, 0, 1, 1, 1, ...

We use the standard infinite geometric series identity: $\frac{1}{1-x} = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \dots$

(i) Sequence: 3, -3, 3, -3, 3, -3, ...

\begin{align*} G(x) &= 3 - 3x + 3x^2 - 3x^3 + \dots \\ &= 3(1 - x + x^2 - x^3 + \dots) \\ &= 3 \left( \frac{1}{1 - (-x)} \right) = \mathbf{\frac{3}{1+x}} \end{align*}

(ii) Sequence: 1, 0, -1, 0, 1, 0, -1, 0, ...

\begin{align*} G(x) &= 1 + 0x - x^2 + 0x^3 + x^4 + 0x^5 - x^6 + \dots \\ &= 1 - x^2 + x^4 - x^6 + \dots \end{align*}

Substitute $u = x^2$:

$$G(x) = 1 - u + u^2 - u^3 + \dots = \frac{1}{1+u} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{1+x^2}}$$

(iii) Sequence: 0, 0, 1, 1, 1, ...

\begin{align*} G(x) &= 0 + 0x + 1x^2 + 1x^3 + 1x^4 + \dots \\ &= x^2 (1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \dots) \\ &= \mathbf{\frac{x^2}{1-x}} \end{align*}

Problem: Solve the recurrence relation $a_{n+2} - 5a_{n+1} + 6a_n = 0$, $a_0 = 1$, $a_1 = -1$.

The characteristic equation is:

$r^2 - 5r + 6 = 0$

Solving for the roots:

\begin{align*} (r - 2)(r - 3) &= 0 \\ r_1 = 2, \quad r_2 &= 3 \end{align*}

The general solution is of the form $a_n = A(r_1)^n + B(r_2)^n$:

$a_n = A(2)^n + B(3)^n$

Apply the initial conditions to find constants $A$ and $B$:

\begin{align*} n=0: \quad a_0 &= A(2)^0 + B(3)^0 = A + B = 1 \quad \implies B = 1 - A \quad \text{--- (1)} \\ n=1: \quad a_1 &= A(2)^1 + B(3)^1 = 2A + 3B = -1 \quad \text{--- (2)} \end{align*}

Substitute (1) into (2):

\begin{align*} 2A + 3(1 - A) &= -1 \\ 2A + 3 - 3A &= -1 \\ -A &= -4 \implies \mathbf{A = 4} \end{align*}

Substitute $A=4$ back to find $B$: $B = 1 - 4 \implies \mathbf{B = -3}$

Problem: Solve using Generating function $a_n = 3a_{n-1} + 2, \quad a_0 = 1$.

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n x^n = 3 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_{n-1} x^n + 2 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x^n$$

Express each sum in terms of $G(x)$:

\begin{align*} (G(x) - a_0) &= 3x \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + 2 \left( \frac{x}{1-x} \right) \\ G(x) - 1 &= 3x \cdot G(x) + \frac{2x}{1-x} \quad \text{[Since } a_0 = 1 \text{]} \end{align*}
