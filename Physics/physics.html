Unit I: Quantum Computing

Question 1(a) 5 Marks

Question: Explain Planck's hypothesis. Deduce the expression of deBroglie wavelength for an electron accelerated through a potential difference of V volt.

Answer:

  • Planck's Hypothesis: Proposed to resolve the ultraviolet catastrophe, it states that electromagnetic energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete, indivisible packets called quanta (photons). The energy of each quantum is directly proportional to its frequency: \(E=h\nu\).
  • de Broglie Wavelength: Extending wave-particle duality to matter, moving material particles (like electrons) exhibit wave-like properties. Their wavelength is inversely proportional to their momentum (\(\lambda=h/p\)).

Derivation:

  • 1. When an electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference \(V\), the work done transforms entirely into kinetic energy (\(K\)):
    $$K = eV$$
  • 2. The classical relationship between momentum (\(p\)) and kinetic energy is derived from \(K = \frac{p^2}{2m}\):
    $$p = \sqrt{2mK}$$
  • 3. Substitute the electrostatic kinetic energy (\(K = eV\)):
    $$p = \sqrt{2meV}$$
  • 4. Substitute this momentum \(p\) back into de Broglie's fundamental wave equation (\(\lambda = h/p\)):
    $$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}}$$
  • 5. Inserting the standard constants (Planck's constant \(h\), electron mass \(m_e\), and elementary charge \(e\)) yields the specific formula for an electron:
    \( \lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å} \)

Question 1(b) 5 Marks

Question: Differentiate Bit and Qubit. Describe the term Quantum entanglement.

Answer:

  • Quantum Entanglement: A deeply non-classical phenomenon where particles interact such that their quantum states become universally linked. Because their states are shared, measuring the exact state of one particle instantaneously forces its entangled partner to collapse into a defined state, regardless of the spatial distance separating them.

Difference: Bit vs Qubit

  • State: A classical Bit is strictly deterministic and binary (always exactly 0 or 1). A Qubit exists in a coherent linear superposition of both states simultaneously (\(\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\)).
  • Capacity & Processing: A system of \(n\) bits stores exactly 1 state at a time. A system of \(n\) qubits can hold and process all \(2^n\) possible states simultaneously, granting exponential parallel processing power.
  • Physical Basis: Bits are physically implemented using macroscopic voltage thresholds in silicon transistors. Qubits rely on delicate quantum properties of subatomic particles, like electron spin or photon polarization.

Question 1(c) 4 Marks

Question: Calculate the de-Broglie wavelengths of a 150g baseball moving at 35 m/s and an electron accelerated through 120V.

Answer:

  • Case 1: 150g Baseball at 35 m/s (Macroscopic)
    • Given: \( m = 0.150 \text{ kg} \), \( v = 35 \text{ m/s} \)
    • \(\lambda = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{0.150 \times 35}\)
    • \(\lambda = 1.262 \times 10^{-34} \text{ m}\)
    • Note: This wavelength is infinitesimally small, showing why wave-particle duality is unobservable in everyday objects.
  • Case 2: Electron at 120 V (Microscopic)
    • Given: \( V = 120 \text{ V} \)
    • Using the shortcut formula: \(\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å} = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{120}} \text{ Å}\)
    • \(\lambda = 1.120 \text{ Å}\)
    • Note: This wavelength is on the order of atomic spacing, which is why electron diffraction occurs in crystal lattices.

Question 2(a) 5 Marks

Question: Show that the energy of a micro particle confined in an infinite one-dimensional potential well of length 'L' is quantized.

Answer: Derivation

  • 1. Consider a particle in a 1D box of length \(L\). Inside the box (\(0 < x < L\)), the potential \(V=0\). Outside the box, \(V=\infty\). Since the particle cannot escape the infinite walls, the probability of finding it outside is zero, forcing the wave function to vanish at the boundaries (\(\psi=0\)).
  • 2. The time-independent Schrödinger's Equation inside the box simplifies to:
    $$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + k^2\psi = 0 \quad \text{where } k^2 = \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}$$
  • 3. The general mathematical solution for this harmonic equation is:
    $$\psi(x) = A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx)$$
  • 4. Applying Boundary Condition 1 (\(x = 0, \psi = 0\)):
    $$0 = A(0) + B(1) \implies B = 0 \implies \psi(x) = A\sin(kx)$$
  • 5. Applying Boundary Condition 2 (\(x = L, \psi = 0\)):
    $$0 = A\sin(kL) \implies kL = n\pi \implies k = \frac{n\pi}{L}$$
    (where \(n\) is an integer \(1, 2, 3 \dots\))
  • 6. Equating our two definitions of \( k^2 \):
    $$\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2} = \frac{n^2\pi^2}{L^2}$$
  • 7. Substituting the reduced Planck's constant \( \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} \), the \(\pi^2\) terms cancel:
    $$E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}$$
  • Conclusion: The energy \(E_n\) is directly proportional to \(n^2\), proving it is quantized. Furthermore, the lowest energy state (\(n=1\)) is non-zero, meaning the particle is never perfectly at rest (Zero-point energy).

Question 2(b) & 2(c) 9 Marks

Question: What is a wave function (\(\psi\))? Explain its physical significance. Calculate the Uncertainty in position of a 50g bullet at 30 m/s (0.01% accuracy) and an electron at 30 m/s (0.01% accuracy).

Answer: Wave Function & Significance

  • Wave Function (\(\psi\)): A complex-valued mathematical function that contains all the dynamical information and fully describes the quantum state of a thermodynamic system or isolated particle.
  • Physical Significance (Born's Interpretation): Because \(\psi\) is a complex number, it cannot be measured directly and has no physical meaning on its own. However, its squared absolute magnitude \(|\psi|^2\) represents the probability density of finding the particle at a specific location in space.
  • Acceptability Conditions: To represent physical reality, \(\psi\) must be single-valued (no multiple probabilities for one spot), continuous (no breaks), finite, and normalizable (total universe probability equals 1).

Answer: Uncertainty Calculation

  • Common velocity uncertainty based on 0.01% accuracy: \( \Delta v = 30 \times 0.0001 = 0.003 \text{ m/s} \).
  • Heisenberg's Formula: \( \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{4\pi} \implies \Delta x = \frac{h}{4\pi m \Delta v} \)
  • 1. Bullet (0.05 kg):
    $$\Delta x = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{4\pi (0.05)(0.003)} = 3.515 \times 10^{-31} \text{ m}$$
  • 2. Electron (\(9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}\)):
    $$\Delta x = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{4\pi (9.11 \times 10^{-31})(0.003)} = 0.0193 \text{ m} \text{ (or 1.93 cm)}$$

Unit II: Optical Fiber

Question 3(a) 5 Marks

Question: Derive the mathematical expression for the numerical aperture and acceptance angle for optical fibre.

Answer: Definitions

  • Numerical Aperture (NA): A dimensionless parameter characterizing the light-gathering capability of the optical fiber.
  • Acceptance Angle (\(\theta_a\)): The maximum angle of incidence (relative to the fiber axis) at the entry face, at which light rays will be successfully guided via Total Internal Reflection (TIR). Note that TIR requires the core index to be greater than the cladding index (\(n_1 > n_2\)).

Derivation:

  • 1. Applying Snell's Law at the external Air-Core interface:
    $$n_0 \sin\theta_a = n_1 \sin\theta_r$$
  • 2. From the right-triangle geometry inside the core (\(\theta_r + \theta_c = 90^\circ\)):
    $$\sin\theta_r = \cos\theta_c$$
  • 3. Applying Snell's Law at the Core-Cladding interface for the critical TIR condition (refracted angle = \(90^\circ\)):
    $$n_1 \sin\theta_c = n_2 \sin(90^\circ) \implies \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$
  • 4. Using the trigonometric identity \(\cos\theta_c = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2\theta_c}\):
    $$\cos\theta_c = \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_1}$$
  • 5. Substitute this back into the first equation (\(n_0 \sin\theta_a = n_1 \cos\theta_c\)):
    $$n_0 \sin\theta_a = n_1 \left( \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_1} \right) = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}$$
  • 6. Final Expressions:
    \(NA = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\)
    \(\theta_a = \sin^{-1}\left( \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_0} \right)\)

Question 3(b) 5 Marks

Question: Classification of optical fibre on index profile.

Answer: Classification

  • Step-Index Fiber: The refractive index of the core is perfectly uniform and drops abruptly (in a sharp step) at the cladding interface. It is cheaper to manufacture but suffers from high modal dispersion because rays reflecting at steep angles take significantly longer to traverse the fiber than axial rays.
  • Graded-Index Fiber (GRIN): The core's refractive index is maximum at the center axis and decreases parabolically towards the cladding. Because light travels faster in mediums with a lower index, rays curving near the cladding speed up, equalizing their travel time with the shorter central rays, effectively minimizing modal dispersion.

Question 3(c) 4 Marks

Question: An optical fibre has a NA of 0.3 and cladding of refractive index of 1.3. Determine the acceptance angle for the fibre in water which has refractive index of 1.33.

Answer: Acceptance Angle Calculation

  • Given: \(\text{NA} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2} = 0.3\), Water medium index \(n_0 = 1.33\).
  • Formula incorporating the surrounding medium: \( n_0 \sin\theta_{aw} = \text{NA} \)
  • $$1.33 \sin\theta_{aw} = 0.3 \implies \sin\theta_{aw} = \frac{0.3}{1.33} = 0.22556$$
  • \(\theta_{aw} = \sin^{-1}(0.22556) = 13.04^\circ \)

Question 4(a) 5 Marks

Question: What is attenuation? Express the various mechanisms of attenuation in optical fibers.

Answer: Attenuation & Mechanisms

  • Attenuation: The progressive reduction or loss of optical signal power as light travels down the length of the fiber. It limits transmission distance and is measured in dB/km.
  • Absorption Loss: Optical energy is absorbed and converted into heat. This occurs intrinsically due to pure silica atomic resonance, or extrinsically due to trapped impurities like OH⁻ (water) ions or transition metals during manufacturing.
  • Scattering Loss (Rayleigh): Deviation of light rays due to microscopic, localized glass density and composition variations that are permanently frozen into the glass as it cools. This is the dominant loss factor at shorter wavelengths (\(\text{Loss} \propto 1/\lambda^4\)).
  • Bending Loss: Radiation of light energy escaping out of the core into the cladding. This happens during macro-bends (when the fiber is physically curved too tightly) or micro-bends (microscopic mechanical stresses acting on the fiber).

Question 4(b) 5 Marks

Question: Describe the application of optical fiber as a sensor.

Answer: Fiber as a Sensor

  • Working Principle: Because light propagation is highly sensitive to the medium, an external stimulus (like temperature, physical pressure, or mechanical strain) physically alters the fiber's geometry or local refractive index. This directly modulates the transmitted light's intensity, phase, or wavelength, which is then measured to calculate the external parameter.
  • Real-World Examples: Fiber Bragg Gratings (FBG) are used as strain and temperature monitors embedded directly into bridges and airplane wings. Hydrophones utilize acoustic waves to alter the optical phase for advanced underwater sonar sensing.

Question 4(c) 4 Marks

Question: Express attenuation in dB/Km, if 15% of the power fed at launching end of 1/2 km fibre is lost during propagation.

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: Length \(L = 0.5 \text{ km}\). Since 15% of the power is lost, 85% successfully reaches the output, so \( P_{out} = 0.85 P_{in} \).
  • Standard Attenuation Formula: \( \alpha = \frac{10}{L} \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{in}}{P_{out}}\right) \)
  • $$\alpha = \frac{10}{0.5} \log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{0.85}\right) = 20 \times 0.07058$$
  • Attenuation \(\alpha = 1.4116 \text{ dB/km}\)

Unit III: Semiconductors

Question 5(a) 5 Marks

Question: Define Electrical Conductivity of metal and derive expression for Electrical Conductivity.

Answer:

  • Electrical Conductivity (\(\sigma\)): The fundamental measure of a material's ability to transport an electric charge. Macroscopically, it is defined as the ratio of current density to the applied electric field (\(\sigma = J/E\)).

Derivation:

  • 1. Consider \(n\) free electrons (charge \(e\), mass \(m\)). The electrostatic force on an electron in an electric field \(E\) is:
    $$F = eE$$
  • 2. By Newton's second law, this induces an acceleration (\(a\)):
    $$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{eE}{m}$$
  • 3. The electrons collide with the crystal lattice but acquire a steady average terminal speed called the drift velocity (\(v_d\)) over a relaxation time \(\tau\):
    $$v_d = a\tau = \frac{eE\tau}{m}$$
  • 4. The electric current density (\(J\)) relates to drift velocity as \(J = n e v_d\). Substituting \(v_d\):
    $$J = n e \left( \frac{eE\tau}{m} \right) = \left( \frac{n e^2 \tau}{m} \right) E$$
  • 5. Comparing this to the microscopic form of Ohm's law (\(J = \sigma E\)) gives the final expression:
    $$\sigma = \frac{n e^2 \tau}{m}$$

Question 5(b) 5 Marks

Question: Discuss the classification of material on the basis of band gap.

Answer: Classification

  • Insulators: Characterized by a massive forbidden band gap (\(E_g > 3\text{ eV}\)). The valence band is completely full, and the conduction band is completely empty. Electrons cannot gain sufficient ambient thermal energy to jump this gap, resulting in zero electrical conductivity at normal temperatures.
  • Semiconductors: Possess a relatively narrow band gap (\(E_g \approx 1\text{ eV}\)). At absolute zero (0K), they act identically to insulators. However, at room temperature, thermal energy is high enough to excite a small fraction of electrons across the gap into the conduction band, allowing moderate, controllable conductivity.
  • Conductors (Metals): The Conduction and Valence energy bands physically overlap, meaning there is no forbidden gap at all (\(E_g = 0\)). The continuous availability of abundant free electrons yields extremely high electrical conductivity.

Question 5(c) 4 Marks

Question: Calculate the drift velocity of the free electrons with a mobility of \(3.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2/\text{Vs}\) in Copper for electric field strength of \(0.5 \text{ Vm}^{-1}\).

Answer: Drift Velocity Calculation

  • Formula relating drift velocity to electric field via mobility: \(v_d = \mu E\)
  • $$v_d = (3.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2/\text{Vs}) \times 0.5 \text{ V/m}$$
  • \(v_d = 1.75 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m/s}\)

Unit IV: Electron Optics

Question 7(a) 6 Marks

Question: Deduce the expression of Bethe's law. Discuss similarities and differences between Bethe's law and Snell's law.

Answer: Bethe's Law Derivation

  • 1. Since the electrostatic field driving the electron acts purely perpendicular to the equipotential boundary separating regions 1 and 2, there is no force parallel to the boundary. Thus, the tangential velocity component remains perfectly constant:
    $$v_1 \sin\theta_1 = v_2 \sin\theta_2 \implies \frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \frac{v_2}{v_1}$$
  • 2. Using the law of conservation of energy (\(\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = eV\)), velocity is proportional to the square root of the regional potential:
    $$v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{2e}{m}V_1} \quad ; \quad v_2 = \sqrt{\frac{2e}{m}V_2}$$
  • 3. Substitute these velocity relationships into the sine ratio:
    $$\frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{V_1}}$$

Answer: Comparison

  • Equation: Snell's Law for photons is \(\frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \frac{v_1}{v_2}\). Bethe's Law for electrons is inverted with respect to velocity: \(\frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \frac{v_2}{v_1}\).
  • Bending Mechanics: Light refracts and bends towards the normal when entering a denser medium where its velocity fundamentally decreases. In stark contrast, electrons bend towards the normal when entering a higher potential region where their kinetic velocity increases.

Question 7(b) 4 Marks

Question: Show that electron describe a parabolic path when it enters in uniform transverse electric field.

Answer: Parabolic Path Derivation

  • This derivation closely mimics projectile motion under gravity.
  • 1. X-axis motion: With no horizontal electric force, horizontal velocity \(v_x\) is constant.
    $$x = v_x t \implies t = \frac{x}{v_x}$$
  • 2. Y-axis motion: The transverse field \(E\) exerts a constant force, giving a constant vertical acceleration \(a_y = \frac{eE}{m}\).
    $$y = \frac{1}{2}a_y t^2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{eE}{m}\right)t^2$$
  • 3. Substitute the time \(t\):
    $$y = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{eE}{m}\right)\left(\frac{x}{v_x}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{eE}{2mv_x^2}\right)x^2$$
  • 4. Result: Since all terms in the parenthesis are constants, let them equal \(K\):
    \(y = Kx^2\)
  • Conclusion: The equation \(y = Kx^2\) is the standard geometric formula for a parabola, proving the path is parabolic.

Question 7(c) 4 Marks

Question: Determine the velocity of ions that pass undeflected through crossed E and B fields for which \(E=7.7kV/m\) and \(B=0.14T\).

Answer: Undeflected Velocity Calculation

  • Condition: For zero deflection in a velocity selector, the opposing Electric and Magnetic forces must exactly cancel out.
  • Formula: \(F_e = F_m \implies qE = qvB \implies v = \frac{E}{B}\)
  • $$v = \frac{7700 \text{ V/m}}{0.14 \text{ T}}$$
  • \(v = 55,000 \text{ m/s}\)

Unit V: Nanotechnology

Question 8(a) 5 Marks

Question: Explain the concept of Nanotechnology.

Answer: Concept

  • Nanotechnology: The multidisciplinary science of controlling and engineering materials exactly at the 1-100 nm scale, where classical physics laws fade and quantum effects dominate material behavior.
  • High Surface/Volume Ratio: As size shrinks to the nanoscale, a massive percentage of atoms reside on the exposed surface. This causes immense chemical reactivity, lowers melting points, and results in extraordinary catalytic efficiency compared to bulk materials.
  • Quantum Confinement: Inside nanoparticles, the continuous energy bands of bulk metals fracture into discrete, quantized energy levels. This fundamentally changes the optical and electrical properties of the material (e.g., gold nanoparticles changing color from yellow to red/purple based purely on particle diameter).

Question 8(b) 5 Marks

Question: Differentiate between Top-Down and Bottom-Up synthesis approaches.

Answer: Synthesis Approaches

  • Top-Down (Destructive): This approach acts like a sculptor, breaking massive bulk materials mechanically or chemically down to nanoscale dimensions (e.g., Ball Milling, Lithography).
    Pros: Highly scalable for heavy industry, fast production.
    Cons: Generates heavy internal lattice stress, impurities, and surface defects.
  • Bottom-Up (Constructive): This approach relies on thermodynamic forces to chemically assemble nanostructures atom-by-atom or molecule-by-molecule (e.g., Sol-gel, Self-assembly).
    Pros: Yields extreme high purity, molecular precision, and zero structural defects.
    Cons: Very slow processing time, expensive and difficult to scale up.

Question 9(a) 5 Marks

Question: Describe Ball Milling and Sol-Gel processes.

Answer: Synthesis Processes Details

  • Ball Milling (Top-Down): A rotating cylindrical drum filled with hardened steel/ceramic balls violently impacts the bulk precursor powder. The continuous high-energy kinetic impacts repeatedly fracture the material into nanoparticles via severe plastic deformation and cold welding.
  • Sol-Gel (Bottom-Up): A multi-step wet-chemical technique. Liquid precursors (metal alkoxides) are mixed with water (hydrolysis) to form a colloidal liquid suspension (Sol). These particles chemically link together (condensation) into an integrated 3D continuous network (Gel), which is subsequently dried and calcined to yield extremely pure nanoscale metal oxides.

Question 9(b) 5 Marks

Question: Discuss the properties of Zeolites and Graphene.

Answer: Special Nanomaterials

  • Zeolites: Microporous, crystalline aluminosilicate minerals possessing a remarkably rigid 3D framework of interconnected nanoscale pores and channels. Their highly specific geometry allows them to act as physical "molecular sieves" and they are used extensively as cracking catalysts in petroleum oil refining.
  • Graphene: A 2-Dimensional single atomic layer of carbon arranged seamlessly in a hexagonal (honeycomb) lattice. Its electrons behave as massless relativistic particles, yielding unprecedented electrical and thermal conductivity. It is highly transparent yet measurably 200 times stronger than structural steel.

Question 9(c) 4 Marks

Question: List the real-world applications of Nanotechnology.

Answer: Applications across Disciplines

  • Modern Medicine: Targeted drug delivery (using engineered gold or lipid nanoparticles to transport highly toxic chemotherapy drugs exclusively into tumors without harming healthy cells) and highly sensitive nano-biosensors for early disease detection.
  • Next-Gen Electronics: Commercial Quantum Dot displays (QLED) producing vibrant colors based entirely on particle diameter, and crucial scaling of ultra-fast nano-transistors below 3nm in modern CPUs.
  • Green Energy: Utilization in highly efficient, lightweight flexible solar cells, and advanced reactive catalysts that vastly increase the surface area inside lithium-ion batteries and hydrogen fuel cells.
  • Advanced Materials: Engineering of self-cleaning surface coatings (mimicking the biological Lotus effect), ultra-stain-resistant synthetic fabrics, and ultra-strong, lightweight carbon-nanotube composites deployed in aerospace engineering.

Question: Define wave function and obtain eigen energy for infinite potential well of width 'L'. Show energy is quantized.

Answer:

  • Wave Function (\(\psi\)): A complex function whose squared magnitude \(|\psi|^2\) gives the probability density of finding the particle.

Derivation:

  • Inside the well, \(V=0\). Schrödinger equation:
    $$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}+k^2\psi=0,\quad k^2=\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}$$

  • Result:
    $$E_n=\frac{n^2h^2}{8mL^2}$$
  • Quantization: \(E_n\propto n^2\) (integer), so energy takes only discrete values.
