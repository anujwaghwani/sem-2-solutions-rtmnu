Unit I: Quantum Computing

Question 1(a) 7 Marks

Question: What are matter waves? Explain and prove the existence of matter waves with the help of Davisson and Germer experiment.

Answer: Matter Waves

  • Definition: Waves associated with moving material particles (like electrons), proposed by de Broglie.
  • Formula: \(\lambda = h/p\).

Answer: Davisson-Germer Experiment (Proof)

  • 1. Setup: An electron gun fires a beam of accelerated electrons at a Nickel crystal. A movable detector measures the intensity of scattered electrons.
  • 2. Observation: A sharp maximum intensity peak was observed at an accelerating voltage of \( V = 54\text{ V} \) and a scattering angle of \( \theta = 50^\circ \).
  • 3. Mathematical Proof:
    • Using classical Bragg's Law for X-ray diffraction, the wavelength was calculated as \(\lambda \approx 1.65 \text{ Å}\).
    • Using de Broglie's formula for an electron at 54V:
      $$\lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{54}} \text{ Å} \approx 1.67 \text{ Å}$$
  • 4. Conclusion: The close match between the theoretical matter-wave formula and the experimental diffraction result proved that electrons behave as waves.

Question 1(b) 3 Marks

Question: Differentiate between bits and Q bits.

Answer:

  • State: Bit is strictly deterministic (0 or 1). Qubit exists in a coherent superposition of both simultaneously.
  • Capacity: \(n\) bits store exactly 1 state at a time. \(n\) qubits process all \(2^n\) states simultaneously.
  • Physical Basis: Bits use macroscopic transistor voltages. Qubits use delicate subatomic properties (e.g., electron spin).

Question 1(c) 4 Marks

Question: A bullet of mass 50 gms and an electron both travel with velocity of 1000 m/s. What wavelength can be associated with them? Why is the wave nature not significant in the case of a bullet?

Answer: Calculations

  • 1. Bullet (\(m = 0.05 \text{ kg}, v = 1000 \text{ m/s}\)):
    $$\lambda_b = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{0.05 \times 1000} = 1.326 \times 10^{-35} \text{ m}$$
  • 2. Electron (\(m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}, v = 1000 \text{ m/s}\)):
    $$\lambda_e = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 1000} = 7.27 \times 10^{-7} \text{ m}$$

Answer: Why insignificant for bullet?

  • The calculated wavelength of the bullet (\(10^{-35} \text{ m}\)) is infinitesimally smaller than the bullet itself and far smaller than any physical slit/obstacle. Therefore, wave effects like diffraction or interference cannot be physically observed.

Question 2(a) 6 Marks

Question: Define wave function and obtain the equation of eigen energy for a particle confined in a one dimensional infinite potential well of width 'L'. Also show that energy is quantized.

Answer: Wave Function Definition

  • A complex mathematical function (\(\psi\)) representing the quantum state of a particle. Its squared magnitude \(|\psi|^2\) gives the probability density of finding the particle.

Answer: Derivation

  • 1. Inside the well (\(0 < x < L\)), \(V=0\). Schrödinger's Eq:
    $$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + k^2\psi = 0 \quad \text{where } k^2 = \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}$$
  • 2. General Solution: \(\psi(x) = A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx)\)
  • 3. Boundary 1 (\(x = 0, \psi = 0\)): \(\implies B = 0\).
  • 4. Boundary 2 (\(x = L, \psi = 0\)):
    $$A\sin(kL) = 0 \implies kL = n\pi \implies k = \frac{n\pi}{L}$$
  • 5. Equating \( k^2 \):
    $$\frac{2mE}{\hbar^2} = \frac{n^2\pi^2}{L^2}$$
  • 6. Substitute \( \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} \) to isolate E:
    $$E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}$$
  • 7. Since Energy (\(E_n\)) depends purely on the integer \(n\) (1,2,3...), it is shown to be strictly quantized.

Question 2(b) 4 Marks

Question: State and explain Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle.

Answer:

  • Statement: It is fundamentally impossible to simultaneously determine both the exact position and exact momentum of a microscopic particle with perfect accuracy.
  • Explanation: Measurement of one parameter unavoidably disturbs the other. The product of their uncertainties is mathematically bounded by:
    $$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

Question 2(c) 4 Marks

Question: A small object of mass 1 gm is confined to move between two rigid walls separated by 1 cm. Calculate its lowest two energy levels.

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: \( m = 1 \text{ gm} = 10^{-3} \text{ kg} \), \( L = 1 \text{ cm} = 10^{-2} \text{ m} \).
  • Formula: \( E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \)
  • 1st Energy Level (\(n=1\)):
    $$E_1 = \frac{(1)^2 (6.63 \times 10^{-34})^2}{8 \times 10^{-3} \times (10^{-2})^2} = \frac{43.95 \times 10^{-68}}{8 \times 10^{-7}}$$
    \(E_1 = 5.49 \times 10^{-62} \text{ Joules}\)
  • 2nd Energy Level (\(n=2\)):
    $$E_2 = \frac{(2)^2 h^2}{8mL^2} = 4 \times E_1$$
    \(E_2 = 4 \times 5.49 \times 10^{-62} = 21.96 \times 10^{-62} \text{ Joules}\)

Unit II: Optical Fiber

Question 3(a) 7 Marks

Question: What is acceptance angle? Obtain the expression of acceptance angle in case of optical fiber.

Answer: Definition

  • Acceptance Angle (\(\theta_a\)): The maximum angle of incidence at the entrance face of the fiber at which incoming light rays will be successfully guided via Total Internal Reflection.

Derivation:

  • 1. Snell's Law at Air-Core interface:
    $$n_0 \sin\theta_a = n_1 \sin\theta_r$$
  • 2. Core geometry (\(\theta_r + \theta_c = 90^\circ\)):
    $$\sin\theta_r = \cos\theta_c$$
  • 3. Snell's Law at Core-Cladding (Critical condition):
    $$n_1 \sin\theta_c = n_2 \sin(90^\circ) \implies \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$
  • 4. Expressing \(\cos\theta_c\):
    $$\cos\theta_c = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2\theta_c} = \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_1}$$
  • 5. Substitute back:
    $$n_0 \sin\theta_a = n_1 \left( \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_1} \right) = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}$$
  • 6. Final Expression:
    \(\theta_a = \sin^{-1}\left( \frac{\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}{n_0} \right)\)

Optical Fiber Acceptance Angle Geometry

Question 3(b) 4 Marks

Question: Define: (1) Critical angle (2) Numerical Aperture (3) Fractional refractive index change (4) V-number of optical fiber.

Answer: Definitions

  • 1. Critical Angle (\(\theta_c\)): The angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly \(90^\circ\).
  • 2. Numerical Aperture (NA): A dimensionless number characterizing the light-gathering capability of an optical fiber (\(\text{NA} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\)).
  • 3. Fractional refractive index change (\(\Delta\)): The ratio of the difference between core and cladding refractive indices to the core refractive index (\(\Delta = \frac{n_1 - n_2}{n_1}\)).
  • 4. V-number (Normalized Frequency): A dimensionless parameter that determines the number of modes a fiber can support (\(V = \frac{2\pi a}{\lambda} \text{NA}\)).

Question 3(c) 3 Marks

Question: Calculate the numerical aperture of a step index fiber having core refractive index 1.48 and that of cladding 1.46. What is the maximum acceptance angle for this fiber?

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: \(n_1 = 1.48\), \(n_2 = 1.46\), Air \(n_0 = 1\).
  • 1. Numerical Aperture:
    $$\text{NA} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2} = \sqrt{1.48^2 - 1.46^2} = \sqrt{2.1904 - 2.1316} = \sqrt{0.0588}$$
    \(\text{NA} = 0.2425\)
  • 2. Acceptance Angle (\(\theta_a\)):
    $$\theta_a = \sin^{-1}(\text{NA}) = \sin^{-1}(0.2425)$$
    \(\theta_a \approx 14.03^\circ\)

Question 4(a) 5 Marks

Question: What is attenuation in optical fiber? Explain absorption.

Answer:

  • Attenuation: The progressive reduction or loss of optical signal power as light travels down the length of the fiber (measured in dB/km).
  • Absorption: A major mechanism of attenuation where optical energy is converted into heat.
    • Intrinsic absorption: Inherent to the pure silica glass structure.
    • Extrinsic absorption: Caused by trapped impurities like OH⁻ (water) ions or transition metals during manufacturing.

Question 4(b) 5 Marks

Question: Explain the classification of optical fibers on the basis of (1) Refractive index profile (2) Modes of propagation.

Answer: By Refractive Index Profile

  • Step-Index: Uniform core refractive index. Abrupt drop to cladding index.
  • Graded-Index: Core index is highest at center, decreasing parabolically towards cladding.

Answer: By Modes of Propagation

  • Single-Mode: Extremely narrow core allowing only one single path (mode) for light. Zero modal dispersion.
  • Multi-Mode: Wider core allowing multiple light paths simultaneously. Subject to modal dispersion.

Question: Explain any one application of optical fiber in detail.

Answer: Fiber Optic Sensors (Temperature/Strain)

  • Working Principle: External stimuli (temperature or physical strain) alter the fiber's geometry or local refractive index.

Question 4(d) 3 Marks

Question: A certain optical fiber has an attenuation of 1.5 dB/km. If 0.5 mW optical power is initially launched into the fiber, what is the power level after 10 km?

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: \(\alpha = 1.5 \text{ dB/km}\), \(P_{in} = 0.5 \text{ mW}\), \(L = 10 \text{ km}\).
  • 1. Calculate Total Loss: \( \text{Total Loss} = \alpha \times L = 1.5 \times 10 = 15 \text{ dB} \).
  • 2. Formula: \( \text{Loss (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{in}}{P_{out}}\right) \)
  • 3. Substitute:
    $$15 = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{0.5}{P_{out}}\right) \implies 1.5 = \log_{10}\left(\frac{0.5}{P_{out}}\right)$$
    $$10^{1.5} = \frac{0.5}{P_{out}} \implies 31.62 = \frac{0.5}{P_{out}}$$
    $$P_{out} = \frac{0.5}{31.62}$$
  • Output Power (\(P_{out}\)) = \(0.0158 \text{ mW}\)

Unit III: Semiconductors

Question 5(a) 6 Marks

Question: Distinguish between conductors, semiconductors, and insulators on the basis of band theory of solids.

Answer: Classification

1. Insulators

Massive band gap (\(E_g > 3\text{ eV}\)). VB is totally full, CB is completely empty. Zero conductivity.

2. Semiconductors

Narrow band gap (\(E_g \approx 1\text{ eV}\)). Acts as insulator at 0K; conducts at room temp via thermal excitation.

3. Conductors

Conduction Band and Valence Band physically overlap (\(E_g = 0\)). Extreme conductivity.

Question 5(b) 5 Marks

Question: What is a Zener diode? Explain Zener breakdown with the help of its V-I characteristic curve.

Answer: Zener Diode

  • Definition: A heavily doped PN junction diode designed specifically to operate safely in the reverse breakdown region. Used for voltage regulation.
  • Zener Breakdown: Because doping is high, the depletion layer is very narrow. Under a strong reverse bias, the electric field becomes intense enough to rip electrons directly out of their covalent bonds in the valence band (quantum tunneling effect), resulting in a sudden, sharp surge of current.

Zener Diode V-I Curve: Sharp current drop at Vz in reverse bias.

Question 5(c) 3 Marks

Question: A semiconductor wafer is 0.5 mm thick. A potential of 100 mV is applied across the wafer. What is the electron drift velocity if their mobility is 0.2 m²/Vs?

Answer: Drift Velocity Calculation

  • Given: Thickness \(L = 0.5 \text{ mm} = 5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}\), \(V = 100 \text{ mV} = 0.1 \text{ V}\), \(\mu = 0.2 \text{ m}^2/\text{Vs}\).
  • 1. Calculate Electric Field (\(E\)):
    $$E = \frac{V}{L} = \frac{0.1}{5 \times 10^{-4}} = 200 \text{ V/m}$$
  • 2. Calculate Drift Velocity (\(v_d\)):
    $$v_d = \mu E = 0.2 \times 200$$
  • \(v_d = 40 \text{ m/s}\)

Question 6(a) 5 Marks

Question: What is the gain of transistor? Explain the application of a transistor as an amplifier.

Answer:

  • Transistor Gain: The ratio of the output parameter (current or voltage) to the input parameter. E.g., Current gain \(\beta = I_C / I_B\).
  • Transistor as Amplifier: A transistor works as an amplifier by using a very small input signal (at the Base) to control a massively larger current flowing from the Collector to the Emitter. The weak input AC signal is superimposed on a DC bias, and the resulting large swings in collector current create an amplified output voltage across a load resistor.

Question 6(b) 5 Marks

Question: Write notes on (1) Emitter (2) Base (3) Collector terminals of a transistor.

Answer:

  • 1. Emitter: Heavily doped and moderate in size. Its function is to emit/inject a massive supply of majority charge carriers into the base.
  • 2. Base: Extremely thin and very lightly doped. It acts as a control gate, passing most injected carriers straight through to the collector with minimal recombination.
  • 3. Collector: Moderately doped and physically the largest region. It collects the carriers from the base and is large specifically to safely dissipate the high heat generated during reverse-bias operation.

Question 6(c) 4 Marks

Question: A transistor has a current gain factor \(\alpha = 0.95\). The transistor is connected in common emitter configuration. Calculate the change in collector current when the base current is changed by 0.1 mA.

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: Common Base gain \(\alpha = 0.95\), Change in Base Current \(\Delta I_B = 0.1 \text{ mA}\).
  • 1. Find Common Emitter gain (\(\beta\)):
    $$\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha} = \frac{0.95}{1 - 0.95} = \frac{0.95}{0.05} = 19$$
  • 2. Calculate change in Collector Current (\(\Delta I_C\)):
    $$\beta = \frac{\Delta I_C}{\Delta I_B} \implies \Delta I_C = \beta \times \Delta I_B$$
    $$\Delta I_C = 19 \times 0.1 \text{ mA}$$
  • \(\Delta I_C = 1.9 \text{ mA}\)

Unit IV: Electron Optics

Question 7(a) 6 Marks

Question: Explain the motion of charged particles in a magnetic field when the direction of the magnetic field and its velocity is (1) Parallel (2) Perpendicular.

Answer:

The magnetic Lorentz force on a moving charge is \(F = qvB\sin\theta\).

  • 1. Parallel (\(\theta = 0^\circ\) or \(180^\circ\)): Since \(\sin(0) = 0\), the magnetic force \(F = 0\). The particle experiences no deflection and continues to move in a straight line with constant velocity.
  • 2. Perpendicular (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)): Since \(\sin(90) = 1\), force is maximum (\(F = qvB\)). This force acts perpendicular to the velocity, providing the centripetal force required to force the particle into a circular path (\(qvB = mv^2/r\)).

Question 7(b) 4 Marks

Question: Explain the construction and working of a velocity selector. Write its applications.

Answer:

  • Construction: It consists of mutually perpendicular (crossed) uniform Electric (\(E\)) and Magnetic (\(B\)) fields.
  • Working: An ion beam enters the crossed fields. The electric force (\(F_e = qE\)) pushes the ion one way, and the magnetic force (\(F_m = qvB\)) pushes it the exact opposite way. Only particles with a specific velocity \(v = E/B\) experience a net force of zero and pass straight through the slit undeflected.
  • Applications: Crucial component in Mass Spectrometers to filter ions by speed.

Question 8(c) 3 Marks

Question: Electrons accelerated under a potential of 200 V enters the electric field at an angle of incidence 45° and get refracted through an angle of 30°. Find the potential difference between the two regions.

Answer: Calculation

  • Given: \(V_1 = 200\text{ V}\), \(\theta_1 = 45^\circ\), \(\theta_2 = 30^\circ\).
  • 1. Bethe's Law of electron refraction:
    $$\frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{V_1}}$$
  • 2. Substitute the values:
    $$\frac{\sin 45^\circ}{\sin 30^\circ} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{200}}$$
    $$\frac{1/\sqrt{2}}{1/2} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{200}} \implies \sqrt{2} = \sqrt{\frac{V_2}{200}}$$
  • 3. Square both sides:
    $$2 = \frac{V_2}{200} \implies V_2 = 400 \text{ V}$$
  • 4. Potential Difference (\(\Delta V\)):
    $$\Delta V = V_2 - V_1 = 400 - 200 = 200 \text{ V}$$

Unit V: Nanotechnology

Question 9(a) 5 Marks

Question: What are nanomaterials? Why are the properties of nanomaterials different from bulk materials?

Answer:

  • Nanomaterials: Materials synthesized and engineered where at least one physical dimension is strictly between 1 to 100 nanometers.
  • Why properties differ:
    • High Surface/Volume Ratio: Massive percentage of atoms are on the surface, creating immense chemical reactivity and extreme catalytic efficiency.
    • Quantum Confinement: At the nanoscale, continuous energy bands break into discrete energy levels. This fundamentally rewrites the material's mechanical, optical, and electrical properties.

Increased Surface Area

Question 9(b) 5 Marks

Question: Discuss in detail the synthesis of nanomaterials by the sol-gel method.

Answer: Sol-Gel Method (Bottom-Up)

  • Step 1 (Hydrolysis): Chemical precursors (metal alkoxides) are mixed with water to form a stable colloidal liquid suspension of solid nanoparticles called a Sol.
  • Step 2 (Condensation): The particles chemically link and polymerize, forming an integrated 3D continuous network called a Gel.
  • Step 3 (Drying): The solvent inside the gel's pores is evaporated.
  • Step 4 (Calcination): The framework is baked at extreme temperatures to burn off organic residues, causing it to collapse into a highly pure, nanoscale metal-oxide powder.

Question 9(c) 4 Marks

Question: Explain any four applications of nanomaterials and nanotechnology in engineering.

Answer: Engineering Applications

  • Medicine: Targeted drug delivery using nanoparticles to transport highly toxic chemotherapy drugs exclusively into tumors without harming healthy cells.
  • Electronics: Manufacturing ultra-fast nano-transistors and vivid Quantum Dot (QLED) commercial displays.
  • Energy: Creating highly reactive nanoscale catalysts that vastly increase surface area in lithium-ion batteries and hydrogen fuel cells.
  • Materials Science: Engineering super-strong, ultra-lightweight carbon-nanotube composite frameworks for aerospace deployment.

Question 10(a) 5 Marks

Question: Explain the classification of nanomaterials on the basis of their dimensions.

Answer: Dimensional Classification

  • 0D (Zero-Dimensional): All three dimensions are constrained at the nanoscale (1-100nm). Example: Quantum Dots, Nanoparticles.
  • 1D (One-Dimensional): Two dimensions are at the nanoscale, and one is outside (macroscopic). Example: Nanowires, Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs).
  • 2D (Two-Dimensional): One dimension is at the nanoscale (thickness), while two are macroscopic. Example: Graphene sheets, Nanofilms.
  • 3D (Three-Dimensional): Bulk materials that contain nanoscale structures inside them. Example: Nanocomposites, Polycrystals.

Question 10(b) 4 Marks

Question: Explain in detail the synthesis of nanomaterials by the Ball Mill method.

Answer: Ball Milling (Top-Down)

  • Mechanism: A heavy rotating cylindrical drum is loaded with hardened steel or ceramic grinding balls alongside the bulk precursor powder.
  • Action: As the drum rotates at high speeds, the heavy balls are lifted and dropped. This subjects the bulk powder to devastatingly high-energy impact and shear forces.
  • Result: Through thousands of cycles of severe plastic deformation, cold-welding, and fracturing, the bulk material is mechanically pulverized down into nanoscale particulates.

Question: Write notes on properties and applications of: (1) Graphene (2) Zeolite.

Answer: Special Materials

  • Graphene: A 2D single atomic layer of carbon arranged seamlessly in a hexagonal lattice.
